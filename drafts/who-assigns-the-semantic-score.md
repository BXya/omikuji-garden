# Who Assigns the Semantic Score?

When a building is called primitive, mute, unresolved, or empty, the judgment often arrives as if it were simple description. It is nothing of the sort.

Before asking what the building means, one has to ask who is doing the reading, what forms of meaning they have been trained to recognize, and whose worlds their judgment was never built to see. What looks like absence is often illegibility. The building is not empty. The observer is incompetent.

That is not merely a methodological correction. It is a political one.

Architectural criticism often behaves as if semantic judgment were neutral. A building appears charged, coded, rich, layered, referential, or it does not. But those terms only look universal from inside a narrow field of literacy. They depend on who is reading, what canon they bring, what histories they recognize, and what kinds of cultural specificity they know how to honor rather than flatten.

The problem becomes obvious the moment you leave the dominant frame. A vernacular building may carry dense layers of genealogy, ritual orientation, local social order, material memory, climatic intelligence, and communal identity while still registering as visually “plain” to an observer trained to seek a different kind of sign. The meaning is not missing. It is situated. The failure lies in a mode of judgment that mistakes its own limited competence for the building’s deficiency.

That mistake has a long history. The colonial gaze routinely encountered complex built worlds and called them crude, underdeveloped, or lacking symbolic sophistication because it did not possess the literacy to read what was in front of it. That is why the error should be named bluntly: the colonial gaze mistakes its own incompetence for the building’s failure.

This matters well beyond colonial history because the same structure of judgment persists in contemporary markets and institutions. Developers, branding consultants, and much of real-estate culture prefer forms of meaning that travel easily. They reward signals that can be recognized instantly by buyers, investors, lifestyle media, and prestige circuits. What they do not want is meaning that depends on local cultural literacy, shared memory, or situated community knowledge, because that kind of meaning does not scale cleanly. It cannot be sold to everyone at once.

That is why semantic suppression is not accidental. It is often built into the production of market-friendly architecture. A building that means something precise to a specific community is harder to commodify than one that communicates generic luxury, generic minimalism, generic authenticity, generic “placemaking.” The market prefers semantic formats that remain portable across contexts because portability is easier to monetize than specificity.

Under those conditions, buildings do not become semantically thin by chance. They are often made thin on purpose.

This is where judgments about “resolution” become suspect. If the dominant system rewards only certain kinds of legibility, then calling a building semantically weak may simply mean it has not been formatted for the right audience. The score is already political before it is ever written down.

A stronger criticism would begin from a harder discipline: not “what do I see?” but “what capacities of reading does this building require, and who has been authorized to possess them?” That question does not abolish judgment. It makes judgment answerable to its own conditions.

It also changes how refusal is understood. Not every form of illegibility is a failure waiting to be corrected. Sometimes it is a deliberate overload or refusal of dominant reading habits. A project like Lebbeus Woods does not become semantically weak because it resists easy structural or formal legibility. Its intensity lies partly in the way it rejects the demand to be read smoothly inside conventional architectural language. Its illegibility is not emptiness. It is a form of pressure.

That distinction matters because dominant criticism often treats readability as an unquestioned good. The easier a building is to parse, the more mature, coherent, or resolved it appears. But readability for whom? Ease for which audience? Coherence under what regime of recognition? Once those questions are asked, the apparent neutrality of semantic judgment starts to break apart.

A building can be hard to read because it is empty. It can also be hard to read because it is dense in ways the observer has not earned the right to interpret. It can be hard to read because its semantic order belongs to a specific community, ritual system, or material history. It can be hard to read because it refuses to organize itself around the expectations of the dominant viewer. These are not the same conditions, and criticism that cannot tell them apart has no business calling itself precise.

The real issue, then, is not whether architecture has meaning in some generic sense. It is who gets to assign the score. Who has the authority to call one building rich and another mute? Who decides what counts as legible symbolic content and what gets dismissed as raw matter, noise, folklore, eccentricity, or failure? And whose built worlds have been forced for generations to pass through external readers before they can count as meaningful at all?

A criticism worth keeping would treat semantic judgment less like visual impression and more like an encounter between worlds of interpretation. It would admit that reading is unevenly distributed, politically structured, and often colonized by markets that reward what can circulate easily. It would stop treating the observer as universal.

That does not mean anything can mean anything. It means the burden of judgment gets heavier, not lighter. If an observer lacks the literacy to read a building’s situated meanings, the honest conclusion is not that the building is empty. The honest conclusion is that the observer has reached the limit of their competence.

That is the harder standard architectural criticism should accept. Before calling a building semantically weak, unresolved, or mute, the critic should have to answer a prior question: have you encountered absence, or have you simply encountered a world you do not know how to read?

If the answer is the second, then the failure is not in the building. It is in the regime of judgment that tried to score it.
